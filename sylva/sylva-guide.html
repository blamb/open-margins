Grounded in TRU's design framework
Caring
Grounded in Maha Bali's Pedagogy of Care. Sylva's instructor notes include wellbeing prompts ‚Äî low-stakes entry points, acknowledgement of uneven prior knowledge, and reminders to check in with learners who may be struggling.
Connected
TRU's framework insists learners need a sense of belonging. Sylva includes a dedicated community segment type for belonging-building activities ‚Äî especially important in online and first-year courses where isolation is a real risk.
Active
Course design should centre meaningful activity, not passive reception. Sylva's lesson arcs use a structured hook ‚Üí direct ‚Üí active ‚Üí synthesis arc, with mode-specific activities (in-class, online synchronous, online asynchronous).
Open
Sylva is built entirely on openly-licensed OER texts from TRU Open Press. Transparent, accessible, and free. Open education isn't just a resource type ‚Äî it's a value that shapes how knowledge is shared.

Hook
Opening ¬∑ Engagement
Activate prior knowledge and create productive curiosity before new content begins
What it does
The hook is the first segment in every Sylva arc. It surfaces what learners already know (or think they know), creates a genuine question the lesson will answer, and lowers the psychological barrier to engaging with difficult material. A good hook doesn't just warm people up ‚Äî it orients them toward the specific ideas the lesson will develop.
How to engage
1
Read the hook activity and the OER passage it references before class
2
Run the activity as written ‚Äî resist the urge to skip it when time feels tight
3
Note what learners say; return to it at the synthesis stage to show how far they've come
Tips
‚ú¶
Keep it under 10 minutes ‚Äî the hook creates energy, it doesn't consume it
‚ú¶
Think-pair-share and quick polls work especially well for activating prior knowledge with low stakes

Direct Instruction
Content ¬∑ Delivery
Efficient, focused delivery of new concepts grounded in the OER text
What it does
Direct instruction segments are for introducing genuinely new concepts that learners cannot construct from prior knowledge alone. Sylva grounds these in specific OER passages ‚Äî every direct segment quotes the text it draws from. The instructor note will flag if this segment risks running long.
How to engage
1
Identify the 1‚Äì2 key concepts this segment must land ‚Äî don't try to cover everything
2
Use the OER passage as an anchor ‚Äî read a key sentence aloud before explaining it
3
Check for understanding before moving on ‚Äî a quick show of hands or chat response
TRU Design Framework note
10-minute guideline: TRU's design framework, following Mayer's Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning, recommends keeping direct instruction segments under 10 minutes. Sylva's instructor notes will flag when a direct segment risks exceeding this. Break longer explanations into direct + active pairs.

Active Learning
Collaborative ¬∑ Practice
Learners do something with the knowledge ‚Äî discuss, apply, create, debate
What it does
Active segments are the engine of learning. Sylva suggests mode-appropriate activities: in-class (think-pair-share, fishbowl, jigsaw, gallery walk), online synchronous (breakout rooms, collaborative docs, chat waterfall), and online asynchronous (discussion boards, reflection journals, peer review). Each is tied to a specific OER passage.
How to engage
1
Give clear instructions and a specific task ‚Äî vague prompts produce vague discussions
2
Keep the OER passage visible while learners work ‚Äî the text is the anchor
3
Debrief briefly after ‚Äî what did learners notice? what surprised them?
TRU Design Framework note
Activity-focused learning: TRU's framework emphasises "varied individual and collaborative learning activities" as central to course design ‚Äî not peripheral add-ons. Every lesson arc Sylva generates includes at least one substantial active segment.

Community
Belonging ¬∑ Connected
Build shared identity, trust, and a sense of place ‚Äî especially in online courses
What it does
Community segments are a new segment type in Sylva, grounded in TRU's Connected value and the Pedagogy of Care. They create moments where learners relate to each other, not just to the content ‚Äî through introductions, shared agreements, peer recognition, or collaborative norms-setting. They're especially valuable at the start of a unit, in online courses, and with first-year students.
How to engage
1
Don't skip or rush community segments ‚Äî belonging is a precondition for learning
2
In online courses, use name introductions, Padlet walls, or shared interest surveys
3
Invite, don't require ‚Äî connection can't be mandated, but space for it can be made
TRU Design Framework note
Connected: TRU's framework states that learners need "a sense of belonging" and that the institution aims to "foster community and relationships within courses." The Pedagogy of Care (Maha Bali, 2020) argues that care for learners as whole people ‚Äî not just as students ‚Äî is itself a form of instructional design. The community segment type makes this concrete.

What it does
Every Sylva arc ends with a synthesis segment. This is where learners consolidate what they've learned, reflect on what surprised them, and connect the lesson back to the learning objectives. Sylva's synthesis segments are grounded in constructive alignment ‚Äî they set up the assessment step that follows.
How to engage
1
Return to the hook ‚Äî what did learners think at the start? How has that changed?
2
Use an exit ticket, reflection prompt, or one-sentence summary to surface understanding
3
Preview the assessment ‚Äî tell learners how today's learning connects to what comes next

Keep synthesis at the end ‚Äî don't move it earlier even when time is tight
‚ú¶

Constructive Alignment TRU Framework
Constructive alignment (Biggs & Tang) is TRU's first design guideline: learning outcomes, activities, and assessment should be aligned so each reinforces the others. Sylva operationalises this across three steps.
