Learning to live
with uncertainty

Rhizo isn't a quiz tool or a summariser. It's an invitation to do what Dave Cormier calls learning in abundance ‚Äî entering texts from unexpected angles, surfacing what they hide, and building your own positioned knowledge rather than just receiving someone else's.

‚Äî Dave Cormier, Learning in a Time of Abundance (2024)

The ideas behind Rhizo
The community is the curriculum
What you learn depends on who you learn with. Rhizo treats your experience, your community's knowledge, and your own positioning as curriculum ‚Äî not just the text.
Finished products hide the mess
Textbooks are finished products. Before the internet, uncertainty was hidden. Rhizo's activities pull back the curtain on what was edited out.
No single entry point
A rhizome has no beginning and no centre ‚Äî you can enter from anywhere. The door you choose to enter through reveals as much as what's behind it.
Deal with uncertainty, don't solve it
Cormier argues that the goal isn't to resolve uncertainty ‚Äî it's to learn to navigate it well. Some questions don't have right answers; they have better and worse responses.
Getting started
1
Load a text
Select a TRU Open Press book and chapter, paste your own text, or load a web page. The richer the text, the richer the activity.
2
Set a focus (optional)
Add a specific question or angle in the objective field if you want the activity to zero in on something particular.
3
Pick an activity
Choose the activity that matches your mood or your learning goal ‚Äî or pick the one that makes you most uncomfortable.
4
Write ‚Äî don't just read
Every activity has response boxes. The learning happens in the writing, not the generating. Request feedback to go deeper, then save your session.
The eight activities

Each activity enters the same text through a different epistemological door. They're not steps in a sequence ‚Äî use the one that fits where you are.

Weed the Garden
what's missing ¬∑ critical
"What the text couldn't, wouldn't, or didn't say."
What it does
Generates an official-sounding summary of the text ‚Äî then immediately undercuts it by surfacing four kinds of hidden content: what got silenced, what's actively contested, what the text assumes you already believe, and what practitioners know that no textbook can capture. Each "weed" comes with a student prompt and a space for your response.
How to engage
1
Read the official summary critically. Notice how confident it sounds. Then ask yourself: does it match your experience of reading the text?
2
Choose the weed that surprises you most ‚Äî or the one you instinctively want to defend the text against. That's your richest entry point.
3
Write from your own community's knowledge. The "community" weed in particular is asking for what you know that the text doesn't ‚Äî don't defer back to the text.
Tips for better results
‚àø
Disagree with a weed. If a weed feels wrong or overstated, say so in your response ‚Äî that disagreement is itself a form of critical engagement.
‚àø
Use the feedback button sparingly. Write your full response first; the AI feedback is most useful when it has something real to push back against.
‚àø
A richer text gets richer weeds. Short excerpts produce more generic results ‚Äî load a full chapter if you can.

Uncertainty Map
what we can't know ¬∑ wicked problems
"Some questions don't have answers ‚Äî only better responses."
What it does
Surfaces three kinds of deep uncertainty in the text: a Wicked Problem (no correct answer ‚Äî only better or worse responses), Contested Evidence (reasonable people reading the same data differently), and a Values Conflict (where evidence can't adjudicate because the dispute is about what matters, not what's true). Each card presents two genuinely different positions and asks you to locate yourself.
How to engage
1
Resist the urge to find the right answer. Cormier's key insight: these questions are structurally unanswerable ‚Äî the goal is to understand WHY, not to resolve them.
2
Write your position honestly, including the parts you're uncertain about. "I lean toward X, but I can't rule out Y because‚Ä¶" is more valuable than false certainty.
3
Notice which type of uncertainty is hardest for you. Some people find values conflicts easier than wicked problems; some find contested evidence most destabilising. That tells you something about how you know.
Tips for better results
‚àø
Watch out for Cormier's two failure modes: factionalization (picking a side and stopping thinking) and apathy (giving up because nothing is certain). Both are ways of escaping the productive discomfort.
‚àø
Add a fourth uncertainty yourself. After working through the three generated questions, try naming one the activity missed that feels important to you.

Wicked Council
no consensus ¬∑ navigation
"None of them is simply wrong. What do you do with that?"
What it does
Extracts a wicked problem from the text and gives it to five voices ‚Äî a Practitioner, a Researcher, a Community Member, an Outsider, and a Newcomer. Each responds from their own context and stakes. None is the expert with the right answer. You're then invited to navigate ‚Äî not to pick the winner, but to figure out how to act when everyone at the table has something real to say.
How to engage
1
Read all five voices before writing. It's tempting to respond after the first or second ‚Äî resist. The Outsider's and Newcomer's views often reframe the whole problem.
2
Identify which voice you find most credible ‚Äî and why. Then deliberately make the strongest possible case for the voice you're most inclined to dismiss.
3
Your navigation response is not a summary. It's your position ‚Äî where you'd stand at this table, what you'd say, and what you'd need to know to act.
Tips for better results
‚àø
The Community is the Curriculum: bring your own community's experience to the council. Which voice speaks closest to what people you know would say? What would they add?
‚àø
Use this in groups. Assign a voice to each participant and have them argue from it ‚Äî then switch. The activity is designed for community dialogue, not just solo reflection.

Nomad's Entry
your way in ¬∑ rhizomatic
"The door you choose tells you as much as what's behind it."
What it does
Creates five radically different entry points into the text ‚Äî an Uncomfortable Question, an Unexpected Metaphor, a Failure Story, a Historical Rupture, and a Community Practice. Each is a different genre and mode of knowing. You choose one door, reflect on why you chose it, and explore what the other doors might have opened.
How to engage
1
Don't choose the door that feels safest. The doors you avoid reveal as much as the one you pick ‚Äî and the activity's reflection question will ask you about that directly.
2
Sit with your chosen door before reflecting. Let it reframe the text for you. Then write about what you now see differently ‚Äî not just about why you chose it.
3
Revisit a door you avoided. After working through your chosen entry, come back to the one that felt most alien. Sometimes the resistance is the learning.
Tips for better results
‚àø
Run it twice with different texts. The door you habitually choose across topics tells you something about your dominant mode of knowing.
‚àø
In a classroom: have different students choose different doors and compare what they found. The divergence between entries is itself the lesson.

Community Contract
your commitment ¬∑ community
"What you bring to a community of learners is as important as what the text contains."
What it does
Generates a four-part learning contract scaffold ‚Äî what you bring, what you need, what you're willing to have challenged, and what you refuse to accept. After filling in each section, Rhizo writes your contract back to you in your own voice as a formal commitment you could actually share with a learning community.
How to engage
1
Be specific. "I bring experience in healthcare" is less useful than "I bring three years working in rural emergency medicine where these protocols don't apply."
2
Take the "what I refuse to accept" section seriously. This is where your values-anchor lives. Cormier calls this the thing that makes community knowledge trustworthy ‚Äî knowing where your line is.
3
Read the generated contract aloud. Does it sound like you? If it's softened your refusals or generalised your specifics, revise and regenerate.
Tips for better results
‚àø
Share it. The contract is designed to be shown to other learners. Comparing contracts before engaging a topic together can transform how a group learns.
‚àø
Return to it after the learning. Did what you refused to accept hold up? Did what you thought you'd need turn out to be different?

Trust Audit
informed trust ¬∑ epistemics
"Knowing HOW you trust is more important than deciding WHETHER to trust."
From Cormier's book
In Learning in a Time of Abundance, Cormier argues that the key 21st-century skill is not fact-checking ‚Äî it's informed trust: knowing who you're trusting, on what basis, and whether that basis is sufficient for you given your own context and stakes.
What it does
Extracts 3‚Äì4 specific claims from the text and types each one: are you being asked to trust a source, a community, a process, or a person? For each, it maps the basis of that trust, poses three audit questions, and connects the claim to Cormier's informed trust framework. Ends with a synthesis prompt about your overall "trust posture" toward the text.
How to engage
1
Audit the basis, not just the claim. The question isn't "is this true?" but "what is the text relying on to make this credible ‚Äî and is that reliable enough for me?"
2
Practice Cormier's humility move: for each claim, ask honestly whether you have enough context to evaluate it ‚Äî or whether the right response is "I don't know enough to have a strong opinion here."
3
Write the synthesis response last. After auditing individual claims, the overall trust posture question ‚Äî "not whether I trust this, but HOW" ‚Äî becomes much more specific and honest.
Tips for better results
‚àø
Notice the trust type distribution. A text that asks you to trust processes (peer review, replication) is doing something different than one that asks you to trust sources (credentials, institutions) or persons (authors' track records).
‚àø
Don't conflate scepticism with informed trust. The goal isn't to distrust the text ‚Äî it's to understand the basis on which you're extending trust, so you can do so consciously rather than automatically.

Abundance Check
hidden uncertainty ¬∑ finished products
"The internet didn't create uncertainty ‚Äî it revealed it."
From Cormier's book
Cormier argues that before the internet, uncertainty was hidden from us. We only encountered "finished products" ‚Äî the food on the table, the polished textbook chapter ‚Äî with all the mess edited out. This activity finds what the text is hiding.
What it does
Names the "finished product" confidence the text projects, then surfaces four hidden layers: what got cleaned up in the process of knowledge-making (hidden process), who has skin in the game (hidden stakes), what the field actually argues about (hidden dissent), and where this text's claims break down (hidden context-dependence). Ends with a "sit with it" prompt ‚Äî not to resolve the tensions, but to hold them.
How to engage
1
Start with the hidden process layer. This is where your practitioner or community knowledge is most valuable ‚Äî you likely know things about how this kind of knowledge gets made that the text won't tell you.
2
Take the "hidden stakes" layer seriously. This isn't about conspiracy ‚Äî it's about the fact that all knowledge is made by positioned people with real interests. Naming this isn't cynicism; it's epistemological honesty.
3
Write the "sit with it" response as a practice. Cormier calls this learning to deal with uncertainty rather than solve it. The goal is not to conclude ‚Äî it's to articulate what productive discomfort feels like.
Tips for better results
‚àø
Bring your community knowledge to the response boxes. What do practitioners in this area know about the "hidden process" that researchers who wrote the text might not? This is community as curriculum in action.
‚àø
Avoid Cormier's two failure modes: don't slide into cynicism ("all texts are propaganda") or apathy ("nothing is reliable so why bother"). The goal is informed navigation, not nihilism.

Breadcrumb Trail
trace the knowing ¬∑ epistemic transparency
"Show where your claim comes from. Then check where theirs does."
From Cormier's book
Cormier's "Practice 2" in Learning in a Time of Abundance: leave breadcrumbs. Don't just say "I did my research." Show where the claim came from, what type of claim it is, and how far you are from the original source. This activity builds that practice.
What it does
Types 3‚Äì4 specific claims from the text as Fact Claims, Interpretation Claims, Value Claims, or Experience Claims ‚Äî and asks you to trace each back to its epistemic roots. Then invites you to make your own claim about the topic, with your own breadcrumbs: stating the claim, its type, and where it comes from.
How to engage
1
Focus on claim type accuracy. The most common error is treating interpretation claims as fact claims. If the text says "research shows X," ask: what research, interpreted how, by whom?
2
Trace as far back as you can ‚Äî then name where the trail goes cold. That moment of "I can't verify further from here" is crucial epistemological self-knowledge.
3
Build your own claim with real breadcrumbs. The hardest part: being precise about type and source. "I believe X (interpretation claim) based on three years of nursing practice (experience claim)" is far more honest than "I think X is true."
Tips for better results
‚àø
Watch for value claims dressed as fact claims. This is the most common sleight of hand in texts ‚Äî claims about what ought to be presented as claims about what is. The claim type categorisation is where the critical work lives.
‚àø
Use this before writing. Running Breadcrumb Trail on a text you're about to cite helps you understand exactly what you're borrowing ‚Äî and whether you're representing it accurately.
‚àø
Pair with the Trust Audit. Trust Audit asks WHO you're trusting; Breadcrumb Trail asks WHAT TYPE of claim you're accepting. Together they build a complete epistemological picture of a text.
